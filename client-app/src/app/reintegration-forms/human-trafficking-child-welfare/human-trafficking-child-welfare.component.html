Directions to interviewer: It is important to make the client feel as safe as possible. Make sure that you ask questions at a pace that feels comfortable for the client and take breaks as needed. Avoid referring to the client as the “victim” or using terminology that the client might not be familiar with. Remind the person that you are there to help and want to make sure they are safe. Adjust the order of the questions if needed and revisit questions if you need to skip over any. Questions are to be asked to the child. NEVER give the assessment to the child to complete on their own. Please include any collateral (admissions staff, law enforcement, etc.) information in the assessment responses.

Special considerations prior to conducting the assessment:

  1. Talk with Law Enforcement before meeting with child to obtain all information and input possible.
    1. Any information you’re able to obtain and not ask the child is helpful.
  2. Conditions
    1. Everything possible should be done to put child at ease, comfortable furniture, asking child is they are hungry or thirsty, in need of a toilet, etc.
    2. When possible, the Interviewer should be of the same sex as the child and speak the child’s preferred language.
  3. Introduction
    1. Introduce yourself and explain your role.
    2. Make sure child feels comfortable and secure.
    3. Elaborate on the purpose of why we are there and what we are to do.
    4. State “We are here to meet with you because there was a concern that you may be in an unsafe situation. We also want to make sure that you know how to get a hold of someone if you need help in the future” Provide brochure/contact info to child including the PRC hotline number.
    5. Ask about whether child is experiencing any physical pain or discomfort or needs immediate medical attention.
  4. Explanation
    1. Explain how the information gathered will be used
    2. Discuss how some of questions asked may be upsetting or painful and that you will pause and take a break when needed. Remind him/her that they can take their time in answering
  5. Last steps before beginning
    1. Establish a rapport by talking about or doing things that are NOT related to the trafficking experience (e.g. things the child is familiar with, play games, etc.)
    2. Monitor child’s non verbal communication as indication of fear or anxiety or acute tiredness. If this is notes, interview should be suspended or terminated.
  6. Opportunity
    1. Use this opportunity to educate and raise awareness of human trafficking.
